Why 2D SLAM works where the 3D stack stalled

The same Livox Mid-360 lidar feeds both pipelines. The difference is what the rest of the stack does with the cloud. The 3D path tries to solve a 15-state estimation problem on a robot whose wheels lie about where it went. The 2D path throws away the top and the bottom of the cloud, keeps only the closest hit at chair-leg height, and runs the same graph-SLAM that a $500 robot vacuum runs.

Like you're 5: imagine drawing a map of your room with your eyes closed, and a friend whispers what's around you. The 3D way is your friend describing the ceiling, the floor, the lamp, the dog, the chair, all of it — and you have to remember every piece in 3D and track how tilted you are. The 2D way is your friend only describing things between your knees and your hips: chair legs, table legs, walls. That's it. Way less to keep track of, and the map comes out right the first try.

3D stack struggled

FAST-LIO2 tracks 15+ states (x, y, z, roll, pitch, yaw, IMU biases, gravity). RTAB-Map adds visual + lidar loop closure on top. Every extra state is a new way to drift, and the mecanum wheels feed lying odometry into the optimizer.

2D stack working

slam_toolbox tracks 3 numbers: x, y, yaw. rf2o invents odom from successive laser scans — wheels don't get a vote. D435i and WitMotion IMU are turned off. Same algorithm class as a Roborock.

How the 3D cloud becomes a 2D scan — sliced, not averaged

Drag the Z band. Every point inside the band survives; every point outside is thrown away. For each angular ray around the robot, the scan reports the closest surviving hit — not the average. That's the whole "3D → 2D" trick.

So is it averaged? No. It's sliced by Z (top and bottom thrown out), then for each direction the scan keeps the closest thing in that direction. Three obstacles in the same direction at 1.2 m, 1.5 m, and 3.0 m? The scan reports 1.2 m. That's "the first thing the robot would bump into" — which is exactly what 2D SLAM and Nav2's costmap want.

Bin mapping — how points become a /scan

Zoom in on the "for each angular bin, keep the closest hit" step. The lidar always sees the full 360° around the robot — that doesn't change. What the slider changes is the angular resolution: how finely that 360° is sliced into discrete reported rays. The yellow wedge highlights one bin so you can see how wide each slice is at the current resolution.

720 bins (the real launch default) = 0.5° per bin → razor-thin slices, can resolve a chair leg from the wall behind it. 12 bins = 30° per bin → very coarse; one slice spans a huge arc, and if a chair at 2 m and a wall at 4 m both fall in the same wedge the scan reports 2 m (the chair — min wins) and you've lost the wall direction entirely. Try cranking it down to 12 and watch the rays collapse.

Angular wedges around the robot

Each wedge is one angular bin. The angle of every kept point (cyan dots from the previous section) is computed; the point lands in exactly one wedge. Green = the winner of that wedge (smallest range). Faded = losers, discarded by the min reduction. Empty wedges report +inf (no hit in that direction).

One wedge, unrolled — winner takes all

Look at a single wedge end-on. The x-axis is range from the robot (0 m → 6 m); each marker is a point that fell into this wedge. The vertical red line is what gets reported in scan.ranges[i]: the leftmost (closest) marker. Click a different wedge on the left to inspect it.
wedge #0 — 0.0° → 10.0° — 0 points, range = +inf
Why min and not average? Because the robot needs to know the first thing it would hit, not the typical distance to stuff in that direction. If there's a chair leg at 0.8 m and a wall at 3 m in the same direction, averaging gives you 1.9 m — a number that exists in physical reality at exactly nothing. The robot would happily plan a path that crashes into the chair. min is conservative and matches what 2D occupancy grids and Nav2's inflation layer assume.

Final output — what each stack actually consumes

Same room, same Mid-360, same instant in time. The 3D stack (FAST-LIO2 + RTAB-Map) consumes the entire cloud — every dot below, colored by height. The 2D stack (pointcloud_to_laserscan + slam_toolbox) throws away everything outside the Z band, keeps the closest hit per angular bin, and feeds those rays into a 2D occupancy grid. Same sensor, two completely different views of the world.
